More schools move to remote learning as COVID-19 appears to be spreading within classrooms

Arika Herron
Indianapolis Star

At least three Indiana school districts have had to return to fully remote learning already this school year as school leaders say they’re seeing more spread between students, more positive cases and quarantine rates that make it hard to keep doors open.

Shenandoah Community Schools announced this week that it would move to virtual instruction in response to rising cases of COVID-19 among students and nearly one-fifth of its student body in quarantine.

Maconoquah School Corporation has also moved to online learning. By the end of last week, nearly half of its students had either tested positive or been identified as a close contact in need of quarantine.

“Maconaquah Administration has been closely monitoring the impact of positive Covid cases and contact tracing among staff and students,” the district said in a post to social media. “It is important to note that through contact tracing, we are seeing the virus being spread student to student.”

This is a change from last year when most schools and health officials said they were seeing very little spread between students in the classroom.

It’s unclear if the change in how students are getting the virus is because of the more transmissible delta variant that’s driving the current surge in new cases statewide, because of a change in COVID-19 policies at the school level or a combination of both.

Most schools have brought back in-person learning

Masks were required in schools through a state mandate last year and many schools had stricter social distancing policies in place.

This year, most schools have brought all of their students back for in-person learning and many have tried to start the year with mask-optional policies – although many have found that policy is untenable and switch to mask requirements over the last week.

“Administration, staff and our community anticipated a return to normalcy at the start of the 2021-22 school year,” Maconaquah administration wrote. “We recognize the inconvenience and are working diligently to design a plan that will allow us to return and remain in person for the remainder of the school year.  Our goal is for our students to be present in our classrooms each day.”

Last week, Scott County School District 1 also had to move learning back online. 

South Madison Community Schools said a combination of high positive cases and quarantines forced it to move Pendleton Heights High School and Pendleton Heights Middle School to virtual learning until Aug. 30. Over the past week, South Madison has seen 25 cases of COVID-19 at the high school and nine at the middle school, pushing those buildings past the 1% positivity threshold the district set for a return to remote learning.

Of the first 28 cases at the high school, 17 had been identified as close contacts, quarantined and then tested positive a few days later said Superintendent Mark Hall.

Hall said more than 20% of students at each school have been out this week, too, leading the district to make the move to remote learning.

Districts try to keep doors open with student quarantines, positive cases

Other school districts around the state are trying to keep their doors open while contending with hundreds or, in some cases, thousands of students out due to positive cases and quarantines.

Last week, more than 1,000 students in Hamilton Southeastern Schools were out for a positive case, COVID-like symptoms or because they were identified as a close contact. The district serves around 21,500 students.

Brownsburg Community Schools, which serves roughly 10,000 students, had close to 700 students in quarantine after the second week of school. Both districts were among many in central Indiana that moved from a mask-optional policy to a mask mandate.

Roughly 750 students are in quarantine in New Albany Floyd County Consolidated Schools. The school district is made up of about 11,000 students, meaning quarantined students make up about 6% of the student body.

Vaccines are "going to be the difference-maker," Superintendent Brad Snyder told the Louisville-Courier Journal. "I hope that that, in and of itself, will be enough to stave off a closing or a shift to virtual. That's my gut."
